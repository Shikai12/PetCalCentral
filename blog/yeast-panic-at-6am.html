Gus Got an Ear Infection and I Panicked for No Reason

I spent an hour googling 'dog ear infection smell' at 6 AM. The vet took one look and said 'yeast.' I had already imagined the worst. Gus was fine in three days. I was not fine for a week.

I woke up at 5:47 AM because Gus was shaking his head. Not a normal shake. A violent shake. The kind of shake that makes his ears flap against his skull like wet towels. I turned on the light. He looked at me. He looked guilty. Dogs always look guilty when something is wrong. I don't know why. They don't know what they did. But they know something is wrong. And they know you're going to panic. And they're right. You're going to panic. I panicked.

I smelled his ear. I know. I smelled my dog's ear at 5:47 AM. This is what pet ownership does to you. It makes you smell things you never thought you'd smell. The ear smelled like bread. Like sourdough bread that had been left in a warm car for three days. It smelled like yeast. I didn't know it was yeast at the time. I just knew it smelled bad. I knew it smelled wrong. I knew it smelled like something that required immediate medical attention. Possibly surgery. Possibly amputation. Possibly the end of the world.

I googled "dog ear infection smell" at 6:02 AM. The first result said "yeast infections smell like bread." The second said "bacterial infections smell like corn chips." The third said "if you smell either, call your vet." I called the vet. I got the answering service. I left a message. I said "my dog's ear smells like bread and he's shaking his head and I think he needs surgery." The receptionist called me back at 7:30 AM. She said "Dr. Martinez can see you at 9." I said "thank you." I said "should I prepare for the worst." She said "it's probably just yeast." I said "what if it's not." She said "then Dr. Martinez will tell you." I said "okay." I did not feel okay. I felt like I was going to vomit. I felt like Gus was going to die. I felt like the $12,000 I had in savings was about to become $0.

6:02 AM: The Google Spiral
I googled for an hour. I found articles about ear infections. I found articles about ear mites. I found articles about ear tumors. I found articles about ear surgery. I found articles about dogs who lost their hearing. I found articles about dogs who lost their ears. I found a forum where someone said "my dog's ear smelled like bread and it was just yeast." I found another forum where someone said "my dog's ear smelled like bread and it was a tumor." I stopped reading forums. Forums are not helpful. Forums are anxiety factories. I closed the laptop. I petted Gus. He shook his head. I cried. A little. Just a little. It was 6:45 AM. I hadn't had coffee.
9:00 AM: The Vet Visit
Dr. Martinez looked at Gus's ear. She didn't even use the otoscope at first. She just looked. She said "yeast." I said "are you sure." She said "yes." I said "how do you know." She said "it smells like yeast." I said "it smells like bread." She said "yeast smells like bread." I said "I know. I read that." She said "then why are you asking." I said "because I'm anxious." She said "I know." She used the otoscope. She looked deeper. She said "mild yeast infection. No bacteria. No mites. No tumor." I said "no tumor." She said "no tumor." I said "are you absolutely sure." She said "I'm absolutely sure." I almost hugged her. I didn't. Professional boundaries. But I wanted to.
9:15 AM: The Prescription
Dr. Martinez prescribed ear drops. $12. Twelve dollars. I had prepared for $2,000. I had prepared for surgery. I had prepared for the worst. The worst was $12. The worst was ear drops. The worst was "apply twice daily for seven days." I said "that's it." She said "that's it." I said "I was ready to spend $2,000." She said "you don't need to spend $2,000." I said "but I was ready." She said "being ready is good. Spending $2,000 is not." I said "fair." I paid $12. I bought the drops. I drove home. Gus shook his head the whole way. I told him "it's okay. It's just yeast." He didn't understand. He's a dog. But he stopped shaking his head for five minutes. Maybe he understood. Maybe he just got tired. Either way, five minutes of peace.
Day 1-3: The Drops
Applying ear drops to a golden retriever is not easy. Gus doesn't like his ears touched. He never has. He flinches. He pulls away. He gives me the look. The look that says "you are betraying me." I have to hold his head. I have to lift his ear. I have to squeeze the drops. I have to massage the ear. He hates the massage. He tolerates it. For three seconds. Then he shakes his head. The drops go everywhere. On my shirt. On the floor. On his fur. I reapply. He shakes again. I reapply again. By day three, I learned to apply the drops in the bathroom. With the door closed. With a towel ready. It's a process. It's a ritual. It's a war. And I'm losing. But the yeast is losing faster.
Day 4-7: The Improvement
By day four, the smell was less. By day five, the shaking was less. By day six, Gus let me touch his ear without flinching. By day seven, the ear was clean. No smell. No redness. No yeast. Dr. Martinez said "all clear" at the follow-up. I said "that was easy." She said "yeast infections are usually easy." I said "then why did I panic." She said "because you're a pet owner. Pet owners panic. It's part of the job." I said "I don't want to panic." She said "then stop googling at 6 AM." I said "I can't stop." She said "I know." She smiled. I smiled. Gus wagged his tail. He was fine. He was always going to be fine. I just didn't know that. I never know that. That's the problem. That's the panic. That's the 6 AM Google spiral.

What I learned: not every smell is a tumor. Not every head shake is a neurological disorder. Not every 6 AM panic requires a $2,000 emergency fund. Sometimes it's just yeast. Sometimes it's just $12 ear drops. Sometimes it's just a week of applying drops and massaging ears and getting drops on your shirt. Sometimes it's simple. But simple doesn't feel simple at 6 AM. Simple feels like the end of the world at 6 AM. Because 6 AM is not a rational time. 6 AM is a time for panic. 6 AM is a time for googling. 6 AM is a time for imagining the worst. The worst is rarely real. The worst is usually just yeast.

I built the Dog Calorie Calculator because of moments like this. Not because ear infections have anything to do with calories. But because anxiety has everything to do with data. When I have data, I'm less anxious. When I know the numbers, I'm less panicked. When I can calculate something, I feel in control. The calculator doesn't help with ear infections. But it helps with the anxiety that causes the 6 AM googling. It gives me numbers. Numbers are calm. Numbers are rational. Numbers are the opposite of 6 AM panic. Numbers are my therapy. And therapy is cheaper than $2,000 emergency vet visits for yeast infections.

Gus is fine now. His ear is clean. His head shakes are normal. Normal head shakes are different from yeast head shakes. I've learned the difference. Yeast head shakes are violent. Normal head shakes are casual. Yeast head shakes happen at 5 AM. Normal head shakes happen after baths. Yeast head shakes make you panic. Normal head shakes make you say "good boy, shake it off." I've learned. I've grown. I've spent $12 on ear drops. I've spent $0 on surgery. I've spent $0 on tumors. I've spent $0 on the worst-case scenarios that lived in my head for three hours on a Tuesday morning. The worst-case scenarios were not real. The yeast was real. The yeast was $12. The yeast was treatable. The yeast was fine. Gus is fine. I am fine. Everything is fine. Until the next 6 AM panic. Which will come. It always comes. But now I know: sometimes it's just yeast. And yeast is not the end of the world. Yeast is just bread that grew in the wrong place. And $12 ear drops fix it. Every time.

The follow-up visit was a week later. I was still anxious. I brought a list of questions. "Could the yeast come back?" "Should I clean his ears regularly?" "What if I miss the signs next time?" "Is there a calculator for ear infection probability?" Dr. Martinez said "no, there is no calculator for ear infection probability." I said "there should be." She said "life doesn't have calculators for everything." I said "it should." She said "you're an IT guy. You think everything can be calculated." I said "everything can be calculated." She said "love can't be calculated." I said "fair." I said "but ear infections can." She said "ear infections can be treated. That's enough." I said "it's not enough for me." She said "I know. That's why you built this website." I said "yes." She said "the website is good. The anxiety is less good. But the website is good." I said "thank you." Gus wagged his tail. He was happy. He was healthy. He was $12 worth of happy. That's the best kind of happy. The cheap kind. The treatable kind. The kind that doesn't require a spreadsheet. But gets one anyway. Because I'm me. Because I track everything. Because tracking is how I love. Because love is just attention. And attention is just data. And data is everything. And Gus is everything. And his ear is clean. And the yeast is gone. And the panic is gone. Until next time. But next time, I'll know. Next time, I'll remember. Next time, I'll say "it's probably just yeast." And maybe I'll believe it. Probably not. But maybe. And maybe is enough. Maybe is hope. Maybe is 6 AM without panic. Maybe is the goal. Maybe is everything.
