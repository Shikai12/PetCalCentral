The Vet Said Gus Is Fat. I'm Still Processing.

'He's a bit... fluffy.' Ideal weight is 75. He was 82. I nodded like I understood. I did not understand.

"He's a bit... fluffy."

That's what Dr. Martinez said. Not fat. Not overweight. Fluffy. Like he's a pillow. Like he's a cloud. Like he's not a 78-pound golden retriever who should weigh 75 pounds but actually weighs 82 pounds and the vet is being nice because she can see the panic in my eyes.

I nodded. I said "okay." I said "we'll work on it." I did not understand what "work on it" meant. I went home and fed Gus the same amount of food. Because I didn't know what else to do. Because the bag said 3 cups for a 75-pound dog. Gus was 82 pounds. I gave him 3 cups. The bag didn't say what to do when your vet calls your dog fluffy. The bag is not a therapist.

Q: What does "fluffy" actually mean?

A: It means your vet is being polite. It means your dog is 7 pounds over ideal weight. It means you need to feed less or exercise more or preferably both. It means the Body Condition Score is probably a 6 out of 9. I learned this later. At the time, I thought "fluffy" was a compliment. It is not a compliment.

Q: How did I not notice?

A: Because I see Gus every day. Because weight gain is gradual. Because golden retrievers are supposed to be big. Because I was comparing him to the golden retriever at the dog park who weighs 95 pounds and his owner feeds him table scraps. I thought Gus was fine because he was smaller than the 95-pound golden retriever. This is not how health works. This is how denial works.

Q: What did I do?

A: First, I panicked. Then I googled "golden retriever ideal weight" at 2:14 AM. Then I found a chart that said 75 pounds. Then I weighed Gus on my bathroom scale. I stood on the scale. I picked up Gus. I subtracted. He was 82. The math was simple. The feelings were not. I sat on the bathroom floor for ten minutes. Gus licked my face. He thought this was a game. It was not a game.

Q: What actually worked?

A: I reduced his food by 15%. I stopped giving him table scraps. I started measuring with a cup instead of eyeballing. I added 10 minutes to his evening walk. I bought a slow feeder bowl so he wouldn't inhale his food and then beg for more. I tracked his weight every Saturday at 8 AM. Same scale. Same spot. Same ritual. The spreadsheet has 26 rows now. He's at 78 pounds. We're getting there. Slowly. Very slowly.

Q: What did I learn?

A: That "fluffy" is a vet's way of saying "your dog is overweight and I'm trying not to make you cry in the parking lot." That I should have noticed the gradual weight gain. That the dog food bag is a guideline, not a rule. That 7 pounds on a dog is like 15 pounds on a human. That I need to be more careful. That Gus is still perfect. Just slightly less fluffy.

Dr. Martinez said "good progress" at the last visit. She didn't say "fluffy." She said "almost at ideal weight." I almost cried. But I didn't. I just nodded. And went home. And updated the spreadsheet. Row 26. Weight: 78.0. Status: getting there. Mood: cautiously optimistic. Gus: still hungry. Always hungry.
