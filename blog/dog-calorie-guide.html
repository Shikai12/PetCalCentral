How Many Calories Does My Dog Actually Need?

May 14, 2026 · 5 min read

I used to just pour kibble until the bowl looked "about right." No measuring. No math. Just vibes.

Then my vet asked me how much Gus was eating and I said "um, a medium bowl?" She just stared at me. Not in a judgmental way. More like a "you absolute disaster" way.

The Problem With "About Right"

Turns out, dogs have actual calorie requirements. Shocking, I know. The same way humans do.

A typical adult dog needs somewhere between 500 and 1,800 calories per day depending on their size, age, and activity level. That's a massive range. A chihuahua and a Great Dane don't eat the same amount. Obviously.

But even within the same breed, there's variation. A couch potato golden retriever eats way less than one who runs three miles a day with you.

The Formula Vets Actually Use

Veterinarians use something called Resting Energy Requirement (RER). It looks like this:

RER = 70 × (weight in kg)^0.75

Then they multiply by an activity factor. Which, honestly, gives me flashbacks to high school algebra. But here's the thing — you don't need to do the math yourself.

That's literally what calculators are for.

What I Did Different

After my "medium bowl" embarrassment, I started actually measuring. Gus gets 2.5 cups of his food twice a day. That's it. No extra treats (okay, maybe a few treats). No "just this once" table scraps.

He's lost four pounds in three months. Not dramatic, but it's working.

The lesson? Guessing is expensive. Not just in food costs, but in vet bills down the road. Overweight dogs have more joint problems, more heart issues, shorter lifespans.
