Is My Cat Overweight? A Practical Guide

May 14, 2026 · 4 min read

Mochi was eleven pounds. The vet said nine. I said "but she's so fluffy." The vet did not agree.

Here's the uncomfortable truth nobody tells you: cats are really good at being overweight. They don't complain. They don't ask for exercise. They just... exist. Adorably. At whatever weight you feed them.

How to Actually Tell If Your Cat Is Overweight

Feel their ribs. Not with a magnifying glass — just run your hands along their sides.

You should be able to feel each rib without pressing. If you need to dig in, there's padding. If you can't feel them at all, that's padding with a capital P.

Other signs:

  • • No visible waist when viewed from above
  • • Sagging belly (not the cute kind)
  • • Grooming becomes half-hearted
  • • They've stopped jumping on things

The Problem With Fat Cats

Overweight cats develop the same problems humans do:

  • • Diabetes (super common in chunky cats)
  • • Joint problems
  • • Shorter lifespan (studies show up to 2-3 years)
  • • Grooming issues → skin problems

I didn't believe it until I saw the statistics. But vets aren't lying. Mochi's vet had charts. Charts!

What Actually Worked

Two things that actually made a difference for us:

1. Measured portions. I bought a little scoop. One scoop in the morning, one at night. That's it. No free-feeding.

2. Play time. Fifteen minutes of wand toy before dinner. She's basically a tiny tiger. She needs to hunt.

It's been eight months. Mochi is nine pounds now. The vet actually smiled at her last visit. I've never gotten a smile before.

It's not about vanity. It's about years. The average indoor cat lives 12-18 years. Overweight cats? Less. A lot less sometimes.
