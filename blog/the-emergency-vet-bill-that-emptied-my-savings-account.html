The Emergency Vet Bill That Emptied My Savings Account

The Emergency Vet Bill That Emptied My Savings Account

I was watching the Broncos game at a bar on Colfax when my phone rang. It was my neighbor, Mrs. Patterson. She said, "Ethan, you need to come home. Max is not okay." I left my beer on the table. I did not pay. I ran.

Max was my golden retriever. He was six years old. He was healthy. He had been to the vet three months earlier for his annual checkup and everything was fine. But when I got home, he was lying on the kitchen floor, panting hard, his gums pale, his abdomen swollen like a basketball. I knew what it was. I had read about it. I had warned other dog owners about it. And now it was happening to my dog.

Bloat. Gastric dilatation-volvulus. The stomach fills with gas, twists on its axis, cuts off blood supply to the stomach and spleen, and within hours, the dog is dead. It is the number one killer of large, deep-chested breeds. And Max had it.

I lifted him into the car. He whined. He was 85 pounds and I am not a big guy, but adrenaline makes you strong. I drove to the emergency vet on Quebec Street — the one that is open 24 hours, the one that costs three times what your regular vet charges, the one that you pray you never need. I carried him inside. The tech took one look and said, "GDV. We need to go now."

They took him to the back. I filled out paperwork. I gave them my credit card. I signed consent forms. The vet came out — a young woman with dark circles under her eyes who had probably been working for twelve hours already. She said, "We need to do surgery. The stomach is twisted. We need to untwist it, assess the tissue viability, and perform a gastropexy to prevent it from happening again. The estimate is $4,500 to $6,500."

I had $6,200 in my savings account. I had been saving for a down payment on a house. I had been saving for two years. And I was about to spend all of it on a surgery that might not work.

I said yes. What else could I say? He was my dog. He was my best friend. He was the reason I got up in the morning when I was depressed. He was the reason I went for walks when I would rather stay inside. He was the reason I talked to people at the dog park when I was too anxious to talk to anyone else. He was not a pet. He was my family. And you do not let family die because of money.

The surgery took four hours. I sat in the waiting room. I did not watch TV. I did not look at my phone. I stared at the wall and I counted the tiles. There were 147. I counted them three times. At hour three, a tech came out and said, "The stomach was viable. We were able to save it. He is in recovery." I cried. I cried in the waiting room of an emergency vet at 2 AM, surrounded by other people who were also crying, and nobody judged me because we were all in the same hell.

The final bill was $6,180. Surgery: $3,200. Anesthesia: $800. Hospitalization: $1,200. Medications: $400. Diagnostic imaging: $380. Aftercare supplies: $200. My savings account had $20 left. Twenty dollars. I had spent two years of saving in one night. And I would do it again.

But here is the thing: I should not have had to. I am a veterinary technician. I work at a clinic in Denver. I see emergencies every week. I see people who cannot afford treatment. I see people who put their pets down because they do not have $6,000. And I had always told myself that would never be me. I had a savings account. I had a plan. I was responsible.

But responsible is not the same as prepared. A savings account is not insurance. A plan is not a guarantee. And $6,200 is not enough when your dog needs $6,180 in emergency surgery. I had $20 left. What if the surgery had cost $6,500? What if there had been complications? What if Max had needed a second surgery? What if he had needed ICU care for a week? I would have been in debt. I would have been choosing between my dog's life and my financial future. And that is not a choice anyone should have to make.

I bought pet insurance the next week. Not the cheap kind. The good kind. The kind that covers 90% of emergency surgery after a $250 deductible. The kind that costs $48 per month. The kind that would have paid $5,562 of that $6,180 bill. The kind that would have left me with $5,620 in savings instead of $20.

I also started a separate pet emergency fund. $100 per month, automatically transferred from checking to savings. In one year, that is $1,200. In three years, that is $3,600. Combined with insurance, that covers almost any emergency. Combined with nothing, it is still better than nothing.

I tell this story to every client who comes into my clinic. I tell them about Max. I tell them about the $6,180. I tell them about the $20. I tell them about the tiles I counted. And I tell them that pet insurance is not a scam. It is not a waste of money. It is the difference between being able to say yes to treatment and having to say no. It is the difference between sleeping at night and staring at the ceiling, wondering if you made the right choice. It is the difference between keeping your savings and emptying it.

Max is fine now. He is seven. He runs at the dog park. He swims in Cherry Creek. He eats slowly — I bought a puzzle feeder to prevent bloat recurrence. He is happy. He is healthy. He is alive. And every time I look at him, I remember the night I spent $6,180 to save his life. And every time I look at my savings account, I remember that it should not have been that close.

— Ethan, from Denver, where the dogs are loved and the savings accounts are finally insured.
