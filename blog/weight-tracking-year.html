I Tracked Gus's Weight for a Year. Here's the Graph.

Every Saturday at 8 AM, I weigh Gus. Same scale, same spot, same ritual. I have 52 data points. The graph looks like a slow downward staircase with one alarming spike.

Every Saturday at 8 AM, I weigh Gus. Same scale. Same spot in the bathroom. Same ritual. I step on the scale. I note my weight. I pick up Gus. I step on the scale again. I subtract. I record the result. I have 52 data points. One for every Saturday for a year. The graph looks like a slow downward staircase. With one alarming spike in December. I'll get to the spike. The spike is important. The spike is why I don't trust holidays.

📊
The Numbers
Starting weight: 82.0 lbs (March 2025) → Current weight: 78.0 lbs (March 2026). Total loss: 4.0 lbs. Average loss: 0.33 lbs/month. The spike: +2.5 lbs in December. The cause: holiday table scraps. The guilt: immeasurable.

Step 1: The Decision. March 2025. Dr. Martinez said "fluffy." I said "okay." I went home. I stood in the bathroom. I looked at the scale. I decided to weigh Gus every Saturday. I didn't know why. I just needed data. I needed numbers. I needed something to control. The weight was out of control. The spreadsheet would bring it back. Spreadsheets fix everything. Spreadsheets don't fix everything. But they help.

Step 2: The Method. Bathroom scale. Digital. Measures to 0.1 pounds. I weigh myself first. 178.2 pounds. I pick up Gus. He's heavy. He's 82 pounds of golden retriever and enthusiasm. I step on the scale. 260.4 pounds. I subtract. 82.2 pounds. I record it. I put Gus down. He runs away. He thinks this is a game. It's not a game. It's data collection. He doesn't care about data. He cares about breakfast.

Step 3: The First Month. March 2025. Weights: 82.2, 82.0, 81.8, 81.5. Downward trend. I was excited. I told my neighbor. He said "that's not much." I said "it's 0.7 pounds." He said "in a month?" I said "yes." He said "my beagle lost 3 pounds in a month." I said "Waffles is a beagle. Gus is a golden retriever." He said "fair." I stopped telling my neighbor. I just updated the spreadsheet.

Step 4: The Plateau. April-May 2025. Weights: 81.5, 81.3, 81.4, 81.2, 81.3, 81.1, 81.2, 81.0. The plateau. The dreaded plateau. I reduced food by 10%. I added 10 minutes to walks. I replaced treats with green beans. The weight didn't move. I panicked. I googled "dog weight loss plateau." I found forums. The forums said "be patient." I am not patient. I am an IT guy. I want immediate results. I want to refresh the page and see the number change. Dogs don't refresh. Dogs plateau. Then they drop.

Step 5: The Drop. June-July 2025. Weights: 81.0, 80.8, 80.5, 80.2, 80.0, 79.8, 79.5, 79.2. The drop. The beautiful drop. The plateau broke. The weight fell. I don't know why. Maybe the green beans kicked in. Maybe the extra walking added up. Maybe Gus just decided to lose weight. Dogs are mysterious. The spreadsheet is not mysterious. The spreadsheet shows the drop. The spreadsheet is beautiful.

Step 6: The Spike. December 2025. Weight: 79.0, 79.2, 79.5, 80.0, 80.5, 81.0, 81.5, 81.8. The spike. The horrible spike. The holidays. Thanksgiving. Christmas. New Year's. My family visits. They bring food. They feed Gus from the table. I say "please don't." They say "just a little." They say "it's a holiday." They say "he's so cute." Gus is cute. Gus is very cute. Gus is also 82 pounds again because of "just a little." I made a chart. I showed the chart to my family. They said "it's just a holiday." I said "the chart doesn't care about holidays." They didn't understand. I ate pie. Gus ate pie. We both gained weight. The chart recorded it. The chart is honest. The chart is cruel.

Step 7: The Recovery. January-March 2026. Weights: 81.5, 81.0, 80.5, 80.0, 79.5, 79.0, 78.5, 78.0. The recovery. Back to green beans. Back to measured meals. Back to no table scraps. Back to the spreadsheet. The weight came down. Slowly. Consistently. The staircase resumed. The downward staircase. The beautiful, slow, painful staircase. One step at a time. One Saturday at a time. One weigh-in at a time.

What the year taught me: weight loss is not linear. It's a staircase. It's flat. Then it drops. Then it's flat. Then it drops. Then your family visits and you gain 2.5 pounds in a month. Then you recover. Then you drop again. The trend matters. The individual data points don't. The Saturday weigh-ins are just data. The trend is the truth. The trend is down. The trend is good. The trend is slow. The trend is real.

I used the Pet BMI Calculator to track the trend. I input the weight every week. I saw the graph. I saw the line. The line goes down. The line has a bump in December. The line resumes. The line is honest. The line doesn't lie. The line is better than my memory. My memory says "Gus is doing fine." The line says "Gus gained 2.5 pounds in December." The line is correct. The line is always correct.

Dr. Martinez said "excellent progress" at the one-year mark. She said "4 pounds in a year is perfect for a large breed." She said "slow and steady." She said "the chart shows you're consistent." I showed her the spreadsheet. She said "you have 52 data points." I said "yes." She said "most people have 3." I said "I'm not most people." She said "clearly." I couldn't tell if that was a compliment. I chose to believe it was.

The ritual continues. Every Saturday. 8 AM. Same scale. Same spot. Same subtraction. Same recording. Gus doesn't know about the ritual. He just knows that sometimes I pick him up and then put him down and then give him breakfast. He thinks the picking up is part of breakfast. It's not. It's data. But he gets breakfast anyway. Because he's good. Because he's patient. Because he's a golden retriever who lost 4 pounds in a year and doesn't know it and doesn't care and just wants his green beans.

The graph is on my fridge. I printed it. It's a line chart. It goes down. It has a bump. It goes down again. My mom saw it and said "that's very organized." I said "thank you." She said "you should track your own weight." I said "I do." She said "do you have a chart." I said "no." She said "why not." I said "because my weight doesn't matter as much as Gus's." She said "that's not healthy." I said "Gus's health is my health." She said "that's sweet." I said "it's not sweet. It's anxiety." She said "same thing." She's not wrong.

One year. 52 data points. 4 pounds lost. One spike. One recovery. One ritual. One spreadsheet. One dog. One human. One bathroom scale. One fridge chart. One story. This is the story. The story of weighing a dog for a year. The story of learning that consistency beats intensity. The story of learning that data is comfort. The story of learning that a golden retriever doesn't care about your spreadsheet but you care about his health and that's enough. That's the whole story. The graph is still going down. The ritual continues. Saturday is coming. I'll be there. With the scale. With Gus. With the subtraction. With the recording. With the breakfast that follows. Always with the breakfast.
