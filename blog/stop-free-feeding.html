Why I Stopped Free-Feeding Mochi

She screamed for a week. Actual screaming. I wore earplugs. But her weight dropped from 15.2 to 10.2 pounds in a year. Here is the honest, messy story of measured meals and British Shorthair judgment.

I free-fed my cat for six months. That was the first mistake. The second mistake was pretending I didn't notice she was getting round. The third mistake was the vet appointment where Dr. Patel said the word "obese" and I cried in my car for twenty minutes. It was a Tuesday. Or maybe a Wednesday. The days all look the same when you're not sleeping because your cat is screaming at 3 AM.

Mochi is a British Shorthair. I adopted her in June 2024 from a hoarding situation. Forty cats in a two-bedroom apartment. She was used to food being available always, everywhere, in bowls that never emptied. When I brought her home, I kept the bowls full. It felt like kindness. It was not kindness. It was a slow-motion disaster with a fluffy tail.

At her first weigh-in with Dr. Patel, Mochi was 15.2 pounds. Her ideal weight is 10. Dr. Patel didn't say "chubby." She didn't say "a little heavy." She said "obese." She said it the way you say "diabetes" or "malignant." Clinical. Direct. I nodded like I understood. I did not understand. I understood the word but not the feeling. The feeling came later, in the parking lot, when I realized I had done this to my cat. I had loved her into obesity.

Dr. Patel gave me a number. 180 calories per day. That's it. For a 15-pound cat who was used to grazing whenever she wanted. I asked how long the transition would take. She said "two weeks of screaming, then she'll adjust." I thought she was exaggerating. She was not exaggerating. She was, if anything, understating.

I bought a digital food scale that measures to the gram. I measured every meal. I split 180 calories into two feedings: breakfast at 7 AM and dinner at 6 PM. The bowls were empty in between. Mochi did not understand this concept. She had never seen an empty bowl. She interpreted it as a personal attack.

She screamed. Not meowed. Screamed. Actual, sustained, operatic screaming that started at 9 PM and continued until I fell asleep at midnight. Then she resumed at 4 AM. I wore earplugs. I bought better earplugs. I considered noise-canceling headphones. My neighbor texted me "is your cat okay?" I said "she's hungry." He said "she sounds like she's dying." I said "she's not dying. She's just very dramatic." He did not believe me. I barely believed me.

Week one was guilt. Pure, concentrated guilt. Every time she screamed, I felt like I was starving her. I googled "cat screaming hunger" at 2 AM. I found forums where people said "just free-feed, they're happier." I found other forums where people said "free-feeding is why your cat is fat." I found no forums where people said "this gets easier in exactly eight days." I needed that forum. It did not exist. I made a spreadsheet instead.

By day eight, something shifted. The screaming stopped at 10 PM instead of midnight. By day twelve, she stopped screaming entirely. She would sit by the empty bowl and stare at me. It was worse than screaming. It was judgment. Pure, British Shorthair judgment. But she stopped screaming. I removed the earplugs. I could hear again. It was nice.

By week four, she stopped hovering around the kitchen between meals. She started playing with her wand toy again. She jumped on the couch without the little grunt she used to make. The grunt was gone. I didn't realize how much the grunt had been bothering me until it stopped. I cried a little. Not as much as the parking lot cry, but still.

At the three-month checkup, Mochi was 13.8 pounds. Dr. Patel said "excellent progress." I said "she still judges me." Dr. Patel said "cats judge everyone. It's not personal." I felt better. Mochi did not. She hid under the bench for the entire visit. She remembers the scale.

At six months, she was 12.1 pounds. The wand toy sessions got longer. She started running down the hallway at 10 PM for no reason. I don't know why cats do this. I don't think anyone knows. But she was running. She wasn't running before. Before, she waddled. Now she runs. The difference is measurable and also visible and also deeply satisfying in a way I can't explain without sounding like a crazy cat person.

At the one-year mark, Mochi weighed 10.2 pounds. Dr. Patel called it "the most successful feline weight loss I've seen this quarter." I asked if that was a compliment or if she just sees a lot of fat cats. She said "both." I appreciated the honesty. Mochi weighed 10.2 pounds. Her ideal is 10. We're close. We're so close I can almost stop measuring her food to the gram. I won't. But I can almost.

She still sits by the empty bowl sometimes. She still stares. But the staring is shorter now. And sometimes, when she's full, she just walks away and knocks something off my desk instead. That's how I know she's happy. When she knocks things off my desk, she's content. It's her love language. It's also deeply annoying. I wouldn't trade it.

Free-feeding is easy. Measured feeding is hard for two weeks, then it becomes routine, then it becomes automatic, then your cat is healthy and you wonder why you ever thought a full bowl was kindness. It wasn't kindness. It was convenience disguised as love. The real love is the measuring cup at 7 AM. The real love is ignoring the screaming. The real love is the year of consistency that gets your cat from 15.2 to 10.2 pounds. The real love is the vet saying "excellent progress" and meaning it.

Mochi is on the desk right now. She's staring at me. She's probably hungry. It's 10:45 AM. Dinner is at 6 PM. She'll survive. I will too. We both survived worse. We survived forty cats in a two-bedroom apartment. We can survive a measured meal plan.

She just knocked my pen off the desk. She's fine. I'm fine. Everything is fine.
