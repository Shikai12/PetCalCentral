Mochi's First Vet Visit: What Hoarding Survivors Need

Forty cats in a two-bedroom apartment. Zero vet visits. Then Mochi came home with me, and everything changed. Here is what the first year actually cost.

I adopted Mochi in June 2024. She came from a hoarding situation. Forty cats in a two-bedroom apartment in Aurora. I don't know how. I don't want to know how. The rescue organization said "she's shy." They said "she needs patience." They said "she might hide for weeks." I said "I have patience." I said "I have time." I said "I have a spare bedroom." I didn't know what I was getting into. I didn't know that a hoarding survivor cat is not just a cat. She's a cat with trauma. She's a cat with fear. She's a cat who has never seen a vet because the hoarder never took them to the vet. Forty cats. Zero vet visits. That's not a home. That's a disaster with litter boxes.

Mochi hid for three days. Under the bed. In the closet. Behind the toilet. I left food and water. I left a litter box. I sat on the floor and read my laptop. I didn't look at her. I didn't reach for her. I just existed in the same space. On day three, she came out. She sniffed my hand. She ran back. On day four, she let me pet her. For two seconds. On day five, she purred. It was the quietest purr I've ever heard. Like a whisper. Like a secret. Like she wasn't sure she was allowed to be happy. She was allowed. She is allowed. She will always be allowed.

Week two: I called Dr. Patel. I said "I have a new cat from a hoarding situation. She needs a checkup." Dr. Patel said "when can you bring her." I said "tomorrow." Dr. Patel said "tomorrow is fine." I hung up. I looked at Mochi. She was sleeping on the windowsill. She looked peaceful. She didn't know about tomorrow. She didn't know about the vet. She didn't know about the carrier. The carrier was her enemy. The carrier was the thing that took her from the hoarding apartment to the rescue. The carrier was trauma. I had to make the carrier not trauma. I put treats in it. I put a blanket in it. I left it open. She ignored it. She knew what it was. She was smart. She was traumatized. But she was smart.

The vet visit was hard. I put Mochi in the carrier. She screamed. Not meowed. Screamed. The same scream she would later use for hunger. But this was fear. Pure fear. She clawed at the carrier. She clawed at me. She peed in the carrier. I drove to the clinic with a screaming, peeing, terrified British Shorthair in my back seat. I cried. I cried while I drove. I cried because she was scared. I cried because I was scared. I cried because forty cats in a two-bedroom apartment never got this. They never got a car ride to a vet. They never got a checkup. They never got help. Mochi was getting help. But she didn't understand. She just understood the carrier. And the carrier was bad.

Dr. Patel was gentle. She didn't reach for Mochi immediately. She sat on the floor. She let Mochi come to her. Mochi didn't come to her. Mochi hid behind the scale. Dr. Patel said "that's okay." She said "we have time." We had time. We spent 20 minutes in that exam room. Mochi eventually came out. She sniffed Dr. Patel's hand. She let her pet her. She purred. The same quiet purr. The secret purr. Dr. Patel said "she's a good girl." I said "she's the best girl." I cried again. I cry a lot. It's fine. Dr. Patel is used to it. She handed me tissues. She always has tissues.

The checkup revealed everything I expected and some things I didn't. Mochi was 15.2 pounds. Obese. She had dental tartar. Grade 2 out of 4. She had ear mites. Both ears. She had a URI. Upper respiratory infection. Probably from the hoarding. Probably from the stress. Probably from the forty cats sharing the same air. She needed dental cleaning. She needed ear drops. She needed antibiotics. She needed a diet. She needed help. She needed everything. And I was going to give it to her. Every single thing.

The cost was $340 for the visit, bloodwork, and initial medications. The dental cleaning would be $800. I scheduled it. I didn't think about the money. I thought about Mochi. I thought about forty cats in a two-bedroom apartment. I thought about how lucky she was. I thought about how lucky I was. I thought about how we were going to do this together. One vet visit at a time. One medication at a time. One meal at a time. One day at a time.

The dental cleaning was in two weeks. I gave Mochi the antibiotics. I gave her the ear drops. I changed her diet. I measured her food. I cleaned her ears. She hated it. She squirmed. She clawed. But she got better. The URI cleared up in five days. The ear mites took two weeks. The dental cleaning happened. She came home groggy. She slept for 12 hours. When she woke up, she ate wet food. She purred. The loudest purr I'd ever heard from her. Like she felt better. Like she knew something good had happened. Like she was grateful. She was grateful. I was grateful. We were grateful together.

Dr. Patel said hoarding survivors often have chronic issues. Dental disease. URI recurrence. Behavioral problems. Mochi had the dental disease. She had the URI. She didn't have the behavioral problems. Not really. She was shy. She was cautious. But she wasn't aggressive. She wasn't destructive. She was just careful. She was careful because she had learned that the world was dangerous. She was learning that the world could be safe. Slowly. One day at a time. One vet visit at a time. One green bean at a time. Wait, no. Mochi doesn't eat green beans. Gus eats green beans. Mochi eats measured portions of high-quality cat food. And she is grateful. And she is healthy. And she is loved.

I built the Pet Cost Estimator because of Mochi. Because I needed to know what hoarding survivor care costs. Because I wanted to plan. Because I wanted to budget. Because I wanted to be ready. The first year of Mochi's care cost me $1,200. The dental cleaning. The follow-up visits. The food. The litter. The carrier. The treats. The toys. The scratching post. The second litter box. The third litter box. The Feliway diffuser. The calming treats. The vet visits. The bloodwork. The medications. Everything. It was worth every penny. Every single penny. Because Mochi is worth it. Because every hoarding survivor is worth it. Because they deserve a chance. Because they deserve a home. Because they deserve a vet who sits on the floor and waits for them to come out from behind the scale.

Mochi is on the desk right now. She's knocking things off. She just knocked my pen off. There it goes. She's satisfied. She will sleep now. She is healthy. She is 10.2 pounds. She is happy. She is safe. She is loved. She is no longer a hoarding survivor. She is just Mochi. She is just my cat. She is just the best girl. And I am just her human. And we are just fine. Together. Forever. One pen at a time.

If you're reading this because you just adopted a hoarding survivor, here's what I want you to know. The first vet visit will be hard. The carrier will be traumatic. The cat will scream. You will cry. The vet will be patient. The cost will be higher than you expect. The recovery will be slower than you want. But it will be worth it. Every single dollar. Every single tear. Every single 20-minute exam room wait. Because the cat who hides behind the scale today will be the cat who purrs on your lap tomorrow. And that purr is the best sound in the world. It's better than music. It's better than silence. It's the sound of trust. And trust is everything. Trust is why we do this. Trust is why I sat on the floor for three days. Trust is why I spent $1,200. Trust is why I cried. And trust is why Mochi just knocked my pen off the desk again. Because she trusts me enough to be annoying. And annoying is love. Annoying is home. Annoying is everything.

Week three was when Mochi started exploring. She left the spare bedroom. She walked into the living room. She saw Gus. Gus saw her. They stared at each other for ten minutes. Neither moved. It was the most tense standoff I've ever witnessed. Two animals. One room. Zero movement. Then Gus wagged his tail. Once. Mochi hissed. Then ran back to the bedroom. Progress. Slow progress. But progress. The next day, she stayed in the living room for five minutes. The day after, ten minutes. By week four, she had claimed the windowsill as her territory. Gus accepted this. Gus accepts everything. Gus is a golden retriever. Acceptance is his default setting.

The Feliway diffuser was $35. I plugged it in the living room. I don't know if it worked. Mochi seemed calmer. But maybe she was just adjusting. Maybe she was just learning. Maybe the diffuser was a placebo for me. I don't care. I'll take placebo. I'll take anything that makes me feel like I'm helping. The calming treats were $12. She didn't eat them. She sniffed them. She walked away. She is a British Shorthair. She has standards. Her standards do not include calming treats. Her standards include measured meals, vertical space, and the occasional pen to knock off the desk. That's it. That's Mochi. That's my hoarding survivor. That's the best girl.
